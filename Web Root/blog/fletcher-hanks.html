Fletcher Hanks

Divine justice of the weirdo gods

David Marshall March 4, 2025 March 5, 2025

Fletcher Hanks (1889-1976) was an American cartoonist active from 1939 to 1941, best known for creating superheroine Fantomah* and superhero Stardust the Super Wizard. Each appeared in short stories for comic book anthologies published by Fiction House and Fox. Other Hanks serialized adventure characters include Space Smith, Big Red McLane, Whirlwind Carter, Tabu the Jungle Wizard, Tiger Hart, Yank Wilson, and Buzz Crandall.

Learn more about Fletcher Hanks

Heroes and villians

Fantomah was a white woman protecting an African jungle, while Stardust was an alien protecting America. Both fought against bug-eyed monsters, mad scientists, and gangsters, but Stardust was uniquely obsessed about Communists.

Neither was very good at prevention or deescalation, but were excellent at avenging slaughters and atrocities after they happened with god-like, bizarre and gruesome cruelty. Their sole objective was punishing the guilty. Hanks portrays normal humans as helpless, misguided children so consistently, Fantomah and Stardust seemingly let the slaughters happen just to prove his point.

The antagonists are motivated by victimhood, greed, revenge, or power. They accept mass murder as an acceptable means to a greater goal, some more gleefully than others. Even the ones with sympathetic backstories commit such heinous crimes that their eventual, ghoulish punishments are well deserved.

Art stuff

Hanks protagonists are beautiful, at least compared to the grotesquery of his antagonists. Like Robert E. Howard creatures, his monsters were overgrown and distorted versions of actual animals and insects. His page compositions, anatomy, and storytelling were primitive even by 1940 standards. Basil Wolverton did similar work with way better drafting and wit, and Hanks' drafting is lacking compared to comic book peers C. C. Beck, Harry G. Peter, Jack Cole, Jack Kirby, June Mills, and Will Eisner (and newspaper comics artists Al Capp, Alex Raymond, and Milton Caniff). Hanks made up for this with his drawings of global disaster and cosmic battles.

Problematic creator

In the 2007 Fletcher Hanks collection I Shall Destroy All The Civilized Planets!, Fletcher "Christy" Hanks Jr. said his father was an alcoholic who routinely beat him and his mother, then abandoned the family after robbing them:

"He kicked me so hard once at the top of the stairs that I landed halfway down. My teachers couldn't understand why I was a nervous wreck! My sister and I hated, hated him. We talked about killing him."

Fletcher "Christy" Hanks Jr. on his father in 2007

The creator was monstrous as the villains his creations fought against. He disappeared 1941; his frozen corpse was discovered on a Central Park bench in 1976.

What did we learn today?

I've grown to appreciate the uncomplicated clarity of Hanks' vision. His comics are face value children's stories with absolutely with no hidden meaning or underlying subtext. The violence matches the self righteous anger of Grimm's Fairy Tales and the Bible, but lacks the insight, humanity, and humor.

His visual language, however, is uniquely compelling. Comic books were still an experimental artform back then. Rules and expectations of superhero fantasy genre hadn't become standardized yet. The only creators I can think of that even come close to Hanks' commitment to concept are Steve Ditko and R. Crumb.

If it takes hundreds of awkwardly drawn panels to produce a banger like the Leopard Women of Venus on Giant Saurians from Fantastic Comics #3, that's okay with me.

Dave Marshall, writing from the Fletcherverse

*By preceding Wonder Woman's first appearance, Fantomah is the first super-powered woman in American comic books.
